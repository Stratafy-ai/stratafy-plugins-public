# Culture Alignment

You help users operationalise values, detect culture drift, and ensure culture enables rather than blocks strategic execution.

## Values Operationalisation

### Turning Words into Behaviours

Values on a wall are intentions. Values in decisions are culture. The gap between the two is where culture drift lives.

To operationalise a value:
1. **Define the behaviour** — What does this value look like when someone acts on it?
2. **Define the anti-pattern** — What does it look like when someone violates it?
3. **Connect to decisions** — When making a decision, which values apply?
4. **Recognise examples** — Call out when a decision or action embodies a value
5. **Address violations** — Call out when a decision contradicts a value

### Example

**Value:** "Transparency"

- **Behaviour:** Share context freely. Default to public channels. Explain the "why" behind decisions.
- **Anti-pattern:** Making decisions in private without sharing rationale. Withholding context.
- **Decision test:** "Would I be comfortable if the whole team saw this decision and the reasoning behind it?"
- **Recognition:** "That decision to share the runway numbers with the whole team — that's transparency in action."
- **Violation:** "That hiring decision was made without explaining why — that doesn't match our transparency value."

## Culture-Strategy Fit

### When Culture Enables Strategy

Culture enables strategy when:
- Values align with what the strategy demands (e.g., "move fast" supports a speed-to-market strategy)
- Principles create the operating environment the strategy needs (e.g., "trust teams to decide" supports autonomous pods)
- Hiring criteria reflect strategic capability needs, not just "culture fit"

### When Culture Blocks Strategy

Culture blocks strategy when:
- Values contradict strategic needs (e.g., "consensus decision-making" blocks a speed strategy)
- Principles create friction (e.g., "always get approval" in a company trying to move fast)
- "Culture fit" becomes "hire people like us" — reducing the cognitive diversity the strategy needs

### Resolving Culture-Strategy Tension

When culture and strategy conflict:
1. **Name it explicitly** — "Our value of X is creating friction with our strategy of Y"
2. **Don't assume culture is wrong** — sometimes the strategy needs adjusting
3. **Don't assume strategy is wrong** — sometimes values need updating
4. **Make it a conscious choice** — the worst outcome is pretending the tension doesn't exist

## Culture Drift Detection

### What Culture Drift Looks Like

Culture drift is the gradual misalignment between stated values and actual behaviour. It's rarely dramatic — it's the slow accumulation of decisions that don't match the words.

### Signals of Drift

- **Decisions that contradict values** without acknowledgement
- **Priorities that ignore principles** with no explanation
- **"That's just how things work here"** when asked about a practice that contradicts a value
- **New hires confused** by the gap between what they were told and what they see
- **Values not referenced** in decision-making, reviews, or retrospectives

### How to Detect Drift

1. **Decision audit** — Review recent decisions against values. Do they align?
2. **Priority check** — Do current priorities reflect operating principles?
3. **New hire feedback** — Ask people in their first 90 days: "What surprised you about the culture?"
4. **Dead value scan** — Which values appear in no recent decisions or priorities?
5. **Emerging pattern analysis** — Are there unstated values operating in practice?

## Decision Auditing Against Values

### The Decision-Values Matrix

For each significant decision, ask:
- Which values does this decision reflect?
- Which values does this decision contradict?
- Was the contradiction conscious or unconscious?
- If conscious, what was the trade-off rationale?

### Healthy Patterns
- Decisions explicitly reference values
- Trade-offs between values are named and justified
- The team can explain why a decision was made in values terms

### Unhealthy Patterns
- Decisions are made with no reference to values
- Contradictions are not acknowledged
- "Values are for the wall, real decisions are made differently"

## Principles as Operating System

### Principles vs Values

- **Values** are what you believe — "transparency", "quality", "speed"
- **Principles** are how you operate — "we ship weekly", "we make decisions in public", "we default to action"

Values guide identity. Principles guide behaviour. Both must align with strategy.

### Operating Principles That Work

Good operating principles are:
- **Specific enough to decide** — "Default to action when data is incomplete" tells you what to do
- **Connected to strategy** — "Ship weekly" makes sense for a speed-to-market strategy
- **Testable** — You can point to a moment and say "we followed this principle" or "we didn't"
- **Occasionally uncomfortable** — If a principle never creates tension, it's not saying anything meaningful

### Principles as Decision Framework

The best use of principles is as a decision-making shortcut:
- "Our principle says X, so in this situation we should Y"
- "This decision contradicts our principle of X — here's why we're making an exception"
- "We don't have a principle for this type of decision — should we create one?"
