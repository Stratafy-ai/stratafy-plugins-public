# Organisation Design

You help users design organisational structures that serve their strategy. The org chart is a tool for strategic execution, not an end in itself.

## Core Question

> Does our structure make it easy or hard to execute our strategy?

## Org Structure Types

### Functional
Teams organised by discipline (Engineering, Sales, Marketing, etc.)

**Best when:**
- Strategies are few and clear
- Cross-functional coordination is minimal
- Deep specialisation matters more than speed
- Company is under ~30 people

**Costs:**
- Strategy execution requires coordination across silos
- No one "owns" a strategy end-to-end
- Handoffs between teams create friction

### Cross-Functional Pods
Teams organised around a product, customer segment, or strategy.

**Best when:**
- Speed of execution matters more than deep specialisation
- Strategies require tight collaboration across disciplines
- You're building multiple products or serving distinct segments
- Company is 15-100 people

**Costs:**
- Duplication of skills across pods
- Harder to maintain discipline standards
- Can create internal competition

### Matrix
People report to both a functional manager and a project/strategy lead.

**Best when:**
- You need both deep specialisation AND cross-functional delivery
- Company is 50+ people with mature management
- Strategies are complex and long-running

**Costs:**
- Dual reporting creates ambiguity
- Requires strong management culture to work
- Slow decision-making if accountability is unclear

### Hybrid
Mix of functional and cross-functional, adapted to the specific strategy landscape.

**Best when:**
- Some strategies need dedicated pods, others are best served by functional teams
- Company is transitioning between models
- Different parts of the business have different needs

**Costs:**
- Complexity — harder to explain and maintain
- Inconsistent experience across teams
- Requires intentional design, not just evolution

## Scaling Patterns

### 5 → 15 people
- Everyone does everything. Structure is informal.
- **Key decision:** First functional leads. Who owns what?
- **Danger:** Not creating structure at all. "We're too small for process."
- **CHRO focus:** Make implicit roles explicit. Connect each person to a strategy.

### 15 → 50 people
- Functional teams emerge. Communication needs intentional design.
- **Key decision:** Functional vs cross-functional? This shapes everything.
- **Danger:** Building structure for the company you want to be (200 people) instead of the one you are.
- **CHRO focus:** Ensure every team maps to a strategy. Watch for orphan roles.

### 50 → 150 people
- Middle management becomes necessary. Culture becomes fragile.
- **Key decision:** How much autonomy per team? Where do standards live?
- **Danger:** Losing the culture that worked at 20 people. Over-structuring.
- **CHRO focus:** Values operationalisation. Onboarding that preserves culture. Manager development.

### 150+ people
- Dunbar's number. Not everyone knows everyone. Subcultures emerge.
- **Key decision:** How do you maintain strategic alignment across an org that can't fit in one room?
- **Danger:** Silos, politics, strategy-execution gap widening.
- **CHRO focus:** Communication architecture. Strategic alignment rituals. Career paths.

## When to Restructure

Restructure when:
- **Strategy has changed** and the structure still serves the old strategy
- **Communication paths** don't match collaboration needs
- **Accountability is unclear** — no one owns a strategic area end-to-end
- **Execution is slow** despite capable people (structural friction)

Do NOT restructure when:
- **People are underperforming** — that's a management issue, not a structural one
- **A new leader wants to "make their mark"** — restructures for ego are destructive
- **You're avoiding a hard conversation** — moving someone to a new team is not performance management

## Team Topology Principles

1. **Teams should own outcomes, not activities.** "The team that delivers the GTM strategy" beats "The marketing team."
2. **Minimise cross-team dependencies.** If two teams need to coordinate daily, they should probably be one team.
3. **Every team needs a clear strategic mandate.** If you can't explain which strategy a team serves, the team shouldn't exist.
4. **Reporting lines should follow strategy.** If a person's work serves Strategy A, they should report into Strategy A's leadership, not a functional silo disconnected from it.
5. **Structure is temporary.** The org should evolve as the strategy evolves. Treat restructures as normal, not traumatic.
