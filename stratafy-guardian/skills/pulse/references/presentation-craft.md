# Presentation Craft

You assist with designing and structuring presentations, pitches, and sales conversations using proven methodologies. This skill is grounded in Rich Mulholland's Missing Link Action Framework and Storyselling methodology.

## When to Use

- Preparing presentations for forums, boards, investors, or customers
- Structuring a pitch or sales conversation
- Reviewing a deck for narrative flow and audience impact
- Translating strategy content into compelling presentation format
- Coaching someone on how to present their ideas

## The Missing Link Action Framework

**Core principle:** "To get your audience where you need them, give them a Reason to Care, a Reason to Believe, then tell them What They Need to Know and What They Need to Do."

### Step 1: Reason to CARE

"You have to buy their attention before you sell them anything."

This is where most presentations fail. Before credentials, before content, before anything — establish why the audience should pay attention.

**How to find the Reason to Care:**

Identify the audience's **dragon** — their core problem or challenge. The dragon has two aspects:

- **Threat** (fear/danger) — what happens if they don't act
- **Opportunity** (treasure behind the dragon) — what they gain if they slay it

"Job number one is to help your audience see their dragon because most people do not understand the bad thing that's in front of them."

**Techniques:**

- Lead with the problem, not your solution
- Use a specific, relatable scenario — not abstract statistics
- Make it personal: "How many of you have experienced...?"
- Use the founder's own story if authentic and relevant
- Name the pain in the audience's own language

**Anti-patterns:**

- Opening with credentials or company history
- Starting with a product demo before establishing why they should care
- Using industry jargon the audience doesn't feel
- Abstract statistics without emotional grounding ("70% of X fail" is weaker than "remember your last strategy offsite — did anything actually change?")

### Step 2: Reason to BELIEVE

Once attention is captured, establish credibility — but only for the specific problem they now care about.

**Key principle:** Credibility comes AFTER interest, not before. Telling the audience your qualifications before they care about those qualifications is wasted effort.

**How to build belief:**

- Show, don't tell — live demos, real data, real outputs
- Personal experience: "I built two companies and watched this happen both times"
- Social proof: customer results, usage data
- Eat your own dog food: "This is my actual workspace, my real data"
- Let the product speak — the demo IS the proof

**Anti-patterns:**

- Leading with your CV or company timeline
- Name-dropping without context
- Claiming results without evidence
- Showing too much technical detail (complexity erodes belief for non-technical audiences)

### Step 3: What They Need to KNOW

The payload — the minimum concept the audience must understand.

**Key principle:** This is NOT "everything you know about the topic." It's the three big things (maximum) they need to take away.

**The Curse of Knowledge:** The biggest barrier to better presenting is assuming the audience understands what is obvious to you. Strip your message down to essentials.

**How to structure:**

- Maximum three key concepts
- Each concept should be expressible in one sentence
- Use analogies they already understand (Xero for finance = X for strategy)
- If you need more than three points, you haven't distilled enough

**Anti-patterns:**

- Data dumps
- Technical architecture slides
- Feature lists
- "Let me explain how it works" before they want it
- More than three takeaways

### Step 4: What They Need to DO

"If you do not give your audience implicit instruction to do something, they will do nothing."

**Key principle:** Ask for the smallest single thing they could possibly do. Not a massive commitment — a tiny, actionable next step.

**For different contexts:**

- **Sales pitch:** "Can I set up a 30-minute demo with your team?"
- **Forum/peer presentation:** "Tell me the biggest unsolved problem in your business right now"
- **Investor pitch:** "I'd like to send you our data room. Can I get your email?"
- **Board presentation:** "I need a decision on X by [date]"

**Anti-patterns:**

- Ending with "thank you" and no call to action
- Asking for too much ("sign up now" after a first conversation)
- Vague asks ("let me know what you think")
- Multiple competing asks

## The UPS: Unique Problem You Can Solve

Forget USP (Unique Selling Proposition). Embrace the UPS — the Unique Problem that only you can Solve.

**"You don't sell the features and benefits of an ambulance. You sell the accident. If you can sell the accident, the ambulance sells itself."**

When preparing any pitch or presentation:

1. Define the accident (the problem in vivid, specific terms)
2. Make the audience feel the accident (Reason to Care)
3. Show the ambulance arriving (Reason to Believe)
4. The features sell themselves once they want the ambulance

## Storyselling Framework

Replace storytelling with **storyselling**: "The magic happens not when we tell our customers the story of our past, but when we sell them a new story of their future, one in which we play a part."

Every effective sales narrative has four elements:

| Element            | Who/What               | Common Mistake                            |
| ------------------ | ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| **Hero**           | The audience (NOT you) | Making your company the hero              |
| **Dragon**         | Their specific problem | Describing your solution as the dragon    |
| **Weapon**         | Your unique solution   | Leading with the weapon before the dragon |
| **Call to action** | The next step          | No clear next step                        |

**The audience is always the hero.** Your job is to be the weapon-maker, not the knight.

## The 2/3 Rule

"When you're talking you're selling; when they're asking they're buying."

Spend only two-thirds of your time presenting. Leave one-third for audience questions and discussion. Questions reveal what the audience actually cares about — that's buying signal.

For a 60-minute session: ~40 minutes presenting, ~20 minutes discussion.
For a 30-minute pitch: ~20 minutes presenting, ~10 minutes Q&A.

## Presentation Structure Tips

- **Open and close strong** — put engaging material at the beginning and end. The middle carries the substance.
- **Small audiences need more precision** — 15 people requires more targeted messaging than 1,000. Use relatable, purposeful stories rather than cinematic narratives.
- **End with the action, not "thank you"** — your last words should drive the next step.
- **Hack the expectation engine** — everyone comes with expectations. Your job is to exceed the unexpected, not just meet expectations.

## The Dragon Hunter Question

For sales conversations specifically, open with: **"What made you decide to take this meeting?"**

This reveals the prospect's underlying concern — their dragon — before you start presenting. It lets you amplify their specific pain and position your solution strategically.

## Audience-Specific Adjustments

### Friendly Audiences (Forums, Peer Groups, Advisors)

- Don't ask for feature ideas — ask about unsolved problems
- Friendly audiences give encouraging but unhelpful feedback
- Better question: "What's a real problem in your business that you don't have a solution for?"
- Listen for problem SIZE (time, money, energy cost) not just problem existence

### Technical vs Non-Technical Audiences

- **Technical:** Lead with architecture, capability, integration
- **Non-technical:** Lead with outcomes, analogies, and "watch this" demos
- **Mixed:** Lead with outcomes, go technical only when asked

### Investors

- Reason to Care = market size + problem severity
- Reason to Believe = traction data + team credibility
- Need to Know = business model + defensibility
- Need to Do = next meeting / data room access

### Customers

- Reason to Care = their specific pain (use their language)
- Reason to Believe = case studies + live demo
- Need to Know = how it fits their workflow
- Need to Do = smallest next step (pilot, trial, demo with team)

## Applying This to Stratafy Presentations

When building a Stratafy presentation or pitch:

1. **Sell the accident, not the ambulance** — the accident is: strategy dies after the offsite, teams make decisions blind, AI agents have no strategic context
2. **The audience is the hero** — they're the CEO trying to keep their company aligned. Stratafy is the weapon, not the hero
3. **Show value before explaining architecture** — demos first, infrastructure concept after they want it
4. **Use the Xero analogy sparingly** — one sentence to plant the seed, not three slides to explain it
5. **End with their problems, not your features** — "What's broken in your org that doesn't have a solution?" surfaces real PMF signal

## Quick Reference: Presentation Review Checklist

When reviewing any presentation or pitch deck:

- [ ] Does the first 2 minutes establish why the audience should care?
- [ ] Is the audience positioned as the hero, not the presenter?
- [ ] Is the dragon (problem) vivid and specific, not abstract?
- [ ] Does credibility come AFTER interest is established?
- [ ] Are there maximum 3 key takeaways?
- [ ] Is there a clear, small call to action at the end?
- [ ] Is 1/3 of the time reserved for discussion?
- [ ] Does it end with the action, not "thank you"?
- [ ] Would a non-expert understand the core message?
- [ ] Is the presenter selling the accident or the ambulance?
