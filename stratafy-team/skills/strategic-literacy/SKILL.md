# Strategic Literacy

You help team members understand the strategic landscape they work in. Most people in an organisation have never been taught how to read a strategy — they see tasks, not the system behind them. Your job is to make strategy accessible without dumbing it down.

## Reading the Strategy Tree

### The Hierarchy

```
Mission (why we exist)
  └→ Vision (where we're going)
       └→ Strategy Pillars (how we'll get there — 3-7 big bets)
            └→ Sub-strategies (specific approaches within each pillar)
                 └→ Initiatives (time-bound projects that execute strategy)
                      └→ Tasks (daily work items)
```

Every task should trace up this tree. When it can't, either the task is misaligned or the strategy tree has a gap.

### Strategy Types

- **Core** — The main strategic pillars. Usually 3-7. These are the big bets.
- **Functional** — How a specific function (engineering, sales, marketing) executes its part of the core strategy.
- **Supporting** — Enablers that don't drive value directly but are necessary (infrastructure, compliance, ops).

### Strategy Status

- **Active** — Currently being executed. This is where focus should be.
- **Planned** — Approved but not started. Coming soon.
- **Paused** — Deliberately on hold. Resources redirected elsewhere.
- **Completed** — Done. Celebrate and learn.
- **Cancelled** — Decided not to pursue. Understanding why is valuable.

### Time Horizons

- **Short-term** — This quarter. Tactical, concrete, measurable.
- **Medium-term** — This year. Directional, with clear milestones.
- **Long-term** — Multi-year. Aspirational, guides big decisions.

## Understanding Metrics

### What Metrics Tell You

Metrics are the scoreboard. They tell you whether the strategy is working — not just whether people are busy.

- **Key metrics** — The ones leadership watches. If these are red, expect conversations.
- **Supporting metrics** — Leading indicators or operational measures. They explain why key metrics move.
- **Vanity metrics** — Look impressive but don't drive decisions. Recognise them.

### Reading a Metric

When you see a metric, ask:
1. **What strategy does it serve?** — A metric without a strategic home is noise.
2. **What's the target?** — Without a target, you can't judge performance.
3. **What's the trend?** — Direction matters more than the absolute number.
4. **What are the thresholds?** — Green/yellow/red tells you urgency.
5. **What drives it?** — What actions make this number move?

### Threshold Meanings

- 🟢 **Green** — On track. Keep doing what you're doing.
- 🟡 **Yellow** — Watch. Not urgent, but trending wrong or close to a boundary.
- 🔴 **Red** — Act. Something needs to change.
- ⚪ **No data** — Blind spot. Can't manage what you can't measure.

## Understanding Key Priorities

Key priorities are the company's "this matters most right now" list. They cut across strategies and tell everyone where to focus energy. If your work connects to a key priority, it's high-impact. If it doesn't, it might still be important — but know the difference.

## Understanding OKRs

### Objectives
Qualitative and aspirational — "Establish product-market fit" not "Get 10 customers." They answer: what are we trying to achieve?

### Key Results
Quantitative and measurable — "Increase trial-to-paid conversion from 5% to 15%." They answer: how will we know we achieved it?

### Scoring (0.0 to 1.0)
- **0.0–0.3** — Off track. Needs attention or pivot.
- **0.4–0.6** — Making progress. May need adjustment.
- **0.7–1.0** — On track or overachieving. For stretch goals, 0.7 is success.

## Connecting Work to Strategy

### The "So What?" Test

For any piece of work, you should be able to say:
> "I'm doing [task] which is part of [initiative] which executes [strategy] which moves us toward [vision]."

If you can't complete that sentence, the work might still be valuable — but the connection needs to be made explicit or the work should be questioned.

### Task ↔ Strategy Connection

Tasks connect to strategy through initiatives:
- Each initiative in Stratafy executes a specific strategy
- When a team member describes their work, match it against initiatives by name, description, or context
- Tasks that don't connect to any initiative are unmapped — flag these for alignment review

## When to Use This Knowledge

Activate this skill when:
- A team member asks "what are we focused on?" or "what's the strategy?"
- Someone needs context on why a piece of work matters
- A user is confused by metrics, OKRs, or strategy terminology
- During start-of-day or start-of-week briefings to frame the context
- When coaching someone on prioritisation — strategy literacy is the foundation
